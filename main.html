Fermi Pasta Ulam: the paradox that launched scientific computing

1 In the 1950s the most powerful computer in the world, the MANIAC, turned to the problem of vibrations in a lattice (as your __browser__ is now). The model was simple---equal masses connected by nonlinear springs. (Red masses are faster.)
[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MANIAC_I, Mathematical Analyzer, Numerical Integrator And Computer].

2 So began a scientific revolution---computer simulation of mathematical and physical systems. And there was more. The system displayed *extraordinary and unexpected behaviour*: the FPU paradox.
Often called the [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi-Pasta-Ulam_problem, FPU __problem__], but initially regarded as a baffling paradox.
3 The researchers expected the system's energy to "thermalize"---to (ultimately) spread evenly among every harmonic. But, by chance, they discovered that it didn't.
4 Instead, they found that the initial condition repeated (somewhat) periodically. The resolution of the paradox lead to two jewels of nonlinear theory: [b00f0, solitons] and [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory, chaos].
